Module 1: Introduction to Accessibility

Why Accesibility Matters

Learning should be for everyone. Accessibility ensures that people of all abilities—including those with visual, auditory, motor, and cognitive differences—can access, navigate, and benefit from educational content. It’s not just about compliance—it’s about compassion, equity, and empowerment.

What You'll Learn

By the end of this module, you'll be able to:

  • Define what accessibility means in digital learning contexts.
  • Identify the common barriers that learners face.

Understanding Accessibility in Digital Learning

Accessibility in digital learning means designing content, platforms, and experiences that are usable by everyone, including individuals with disabilities. It goes beyond compliance checklists—true accessibility is about removing barriers so all learners can access content equitably,effectively, and comfortably. It ensures that a screen reader can describe what a sighted person sees, that a keyboard can substitute for a mouse, and that no one is excluded based on how they learn or interact with information. Digital accessibility is the foundation for inclusive learning. It doesn’t only benefit those with permanent disabilities—it also supports:

  • Temporary challenges (e.g., broken wrist)
  • Sitauation limitations (e.g., noisy environments)
  • Aging users
  • Neurodivergent learners

Common Barriers Learners Face

You are a person with visual impairment and you are reviewing a course module that includes a pie chart. Your screen reader encounters this chart and simply announces: 'Image.' What will you do?
What is the barrier here?

Despite the best intentions, digital experiences often fall short for many learners. Here's what that can look like in practice.

Common Accessibility Barriers by Disability Type
Type of Disability Barriers
Visual impairment
  • Images without descriptive alt text
  • Poor color contrast between text and background
  • Non-descriptive link labels (e.g., “Click here”)
  • No heading structure for screen reader navigation
Hearing impairment
  • Videos without captions or transcripts
  • Audio-only instructions with no visual alternative
  • Background music that competes with narration
Cognitive or learning differences
  • Dense blocks of text without visual breaks
  • Overstimulating animations or transitions
  • Inconsistent navigation or layout
Motor or mobility impairments
  • Interactive elements that require mouse use
  • Small or poorly placed clickable targets
  • Forms or tasks with strict time limits

Why This Matters?

When these barriers go unaddressed, learners can’t fully engage, complete tasks, or feel confident in their learning environment. Accessibility invites everyone to the table—not just to sit, but to thrive.
